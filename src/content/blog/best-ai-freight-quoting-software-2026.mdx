## The SaaS Quoting Platforms: Wisor, Zauber, and Raft

Three SaaS vendors are the usual shortlist for forwarders buying quote automation off the shelf. Each deploys in weeks, charges a recurring subscription, and works best when your quoting workflow matches the product's template. They differ on mode focus and how much of the quote lifecycle they actually automate.

**Wisor** has built a strong quote automation platform, particularly for air freight. Its advantage is a network of carrier rate connections (Lufthansa Cargo, Emirates SkyCargo, Cathay Cargo) that feed real-time pricing into the quote workflow, with sub-5-minute response times. For air forwarders processing 500+ RFQs per month with standardized margin structures, Wisor offers fast time to value. Its pricing logic is template-driven, so heavy customer-specific margin exceptions are where it strains.

**Zauber** positions itself as a SaaS freight automation product with quoting among its pre-built workflows. It suits small to mid-size operations that want quick deployment, predictable monthly costs, and a managed SaaS experience, provided the workflow fits the product's configuration options. Forwarders with unusual document formats or deep TMS customization needs tend to hit the product's ceiling. Our [Zauber vs. FreightMynd comparison](/compare/zauber-vs-freightmynd/) covers the differences in deployment, ownership, and customization in detail.

**Raft** (formerly Vector.ai) is a VC-backed SaaS platform serving 60+ forwarders across 30+ countries. Its publicly documented strengths are document automation, invoice processing, and email triage rather than quoting as a core engine, so it fits best as the intake layer feeding a quoting workflow. It documents a CargoWise integration, and for standard workflows at enterprise scale it is a credible option.

## Rate Platforms and TMS-Native Quoting: Freightos, Expedock, and CargoWise

Not every quoting problem needs a quoting platform. Some forwarders are better served by a rate marketplace, cleaner document data, or the quoting module already inside their TMS. These three solve adjacent pieces of the workflow, and honest evaluation means knowing which piece is your actual bottleneck.

**Freightos/WebCargo** operates the largest digital freight marketplace, with rate benchmarking through the Freightos Baltic Index (FBX) drawn from bookings across 5,000+ forwarders. For spot quoting and market benchmarking, its data breadth is something no custom system can replicate; if you need to know what the market pays on a lane, use it. It is a rate source, though, not your margin engine. See our [Freightos vs. FreightMynd comparison](/compare/freightos-vs-freightmynd/) for where the approaches diverge.

**Expedock** is a document extraction platform, not a quoting engine, and belongs on this list for one reason: quote quality depends on clean shipment data. Its large training dataset gives good baseline accuracy on standard formats, making it a reasonable upstream feed at 10,000+ documents per month. It will not calculate margins or generate quote documents.

**CargoWise's native rate module** is the default answer for operations that live entirely inside CargoWise: rates, quotes, and shipment records in one system, no synchronization layer. The trade-off is that rates must already be in the module (someone still keys in carrier rate sheets), and RFQ intake from email remains manual. It is quoting infrastructure, not quoting automation.

## When SaaS Is the Right Answer (and When It Is Not)

SaaS quoting software is the right buy when your workflow is standard: consistent margins by lane, RFQs arriving through portals or structured channels, rate sources the product already connects to, and no strong need to own the system. Wisor for air freight and Zauber for general automation deliver value fast in that situation, and Freightos is unmatched for market rate data.

Custom wins when your quoting workflow is your edge. The margin rules you apply, the exceptions you make for strategic accounts, the way you price a lane where competition is thin: this logic is why you win business. A SaaS product that forces it into a generic pricing template can cost more in eroded positioning than it saves in deployment time.

The signals that point to custom:

- RFQs arrive as free-text email in inconsistent formats, and intake is your bottleneck
- Margin rules vary by customer tier, lane competitiveness, and volume, with regular exceptions
- Rates live across carrier portals, spreadsheets, rate modules, and email, with no single source of truth
- Per-seat or per-quote SaaS pricing becomes cost-prohibitive at your volume
- You want the system deployed in your environment, owned outright, with no vendor dependency

Plenty of mature operations combine both: Freightos for market benchmarks feeding a custom quoting engine that applies their own margin logic. The tools are inputs; the pricing decision stays yours.

## How to Choose: A Practical Evaluation Sequence

Start by measuring where quoting time actually goes. Track one week of RFQs: time from receipt to quote sent, minutes spent on rate lookup versus margin calculation versus formatting, and your quote-to-book conversion rate. The step consuming the most time tells you which tool category you need.

Then apply three filters, in order:

1. **TMS integration depth.** Can the tool pull rates from and push quotes into CargoWise, SAP TM, or whatever you run? Test on your instance, not the vendor's demo. A quoting tool that cannot reach your system of record adds a manual step instead of removing one.
2. **Intake fit.** If most RFQs arrive as unstructured email, verify the tool parses your actual emails, including messy ones with follow-up modifications. This is where template-driven products fail quietly.
3. **Margin logic fit.** Write down your five most common pricing exceptions and ask the vendor to configure them live. If the answer involves a workaround, your pricing logic does not fit the template.
